Efficiency in Design

  • Design around getting the most bang for your buck. Think of your game as a car engine. Developer resources are the fuel and the output is the player value. Two engines might not be on the same scale, but the efficiency in terms of resources consumed is radically different.

  • Multiplayer
    Making a single player epic saga with hours of linear consumable gameplay is not very efficient. A well designed multiplayer game that simply gives players a set of rules can provide an infinite amount of gameplay for a fraction of the time spent creating content. as well as dramatically increase a game's lifespan.

  • User Generated Content
    If you don't have the resources to make large amounts of content yourself, why not give players the tools to do it? An even better argument is that it is almost certainty that players will come up with things for your game that you would never have created.

  • Procedural Content
    If done well, the ability to create an endless amount of content for the frontloaded price of designing the generation system can provide for exponential efficiency.

  • Avoid Photorealistic Art Direction
    Any game that utilizes a photorealistic look is immediatly in visual competition with other(better) games, and the amount of resources it takes to compete on their level is growing every year. Typically only the largest publishers have the budget to create these titles.

  • Re-Explore the explored with Modern Tech
    One way to get results with fewer resources is to explore themes and designs from 15-20 years ago with technology from today.

Utilize Open Technologies

  • There is no shame in climbing on the backs of others, especially when you are trying to make the most with the least.
    Check out free or cheap technologies to build off.

  • Rendering - Ogre, Irrlicht, HGE, Panda3d, Crystal Space, SDL

  • Game Engines - Flash 9(AS3), Shockwave, C4, NeoAxis, jME, Torque, Unity, Blitz, id Tech 3, XNA, Multiverse, Adventure Game Studio, Game Maker, Wintermute, Scrolling Game Development Kit, Source, PopCap Framework

  • Physics - Bullet, PhysX, Farseer, Box2D, Motor, Chipmunk

  • Networking - RakNet, SmartFoxServer, HawkNL

  • Scripting Languages - Lua, Python, GameMonkey, Ruby

  • Sound - OpenAL, SDL mixer, DUMB, irrKlang, Audiere, MikMod, BASS

  • GUI - AntTweakBar, Guichan, Navi, LibUFO

Digital Distribution

  • The ability for developers to deliver their games directly into the hands of their audience is an enourmous step forward in terms of efficiency. The resources required to dstribute games over the internet are microscopic compared to the cost of pressing, packaging, and shipping physical copies of games to stores all over the world.

  • When nearing the marketing stages of the development process, start researching the current digital platforms available and start marketing.

Develop on Open Platforms

  • As any professional developer could tell you, getting a game onto closed game platforms can require a lot of resources. New digital platforms on consoles such as Xbox Live Arcade, which serve as more favorable outlet to less resource intensive games, still hold many of the downfalls of a closed platform.

  • By developing your games on open platforms like the computer or web, the resources you would have spent fulfilling the requirements of a closed platform can be saved.

Collaboration

  • An integral part of developing with little resources is collaborating with others who have the resources you need. Indies look for other developers with a similar focus or that might be willing to collaborate or trade in lieu of a more traditioal business deal. Unrestrained by the clout of commercial competition most developers actively participate in online communities and share the most intimate details of their development process.

Less Traditional Monetization

  • There could be more efficient ways to monetize your game other than the traditional means. A game designed to be played for free could still be monetized in a variety of different ways. Advertising, micropayments, virtual item sales or even sell your gae while it's still in development.

  • This is a very delicate solution however and designers should take great care into how these systems are implemented. Don't go crazy here or it could destroy the product.

Redefine Success

  • A game developed with a fraction of the resources of a blockbuster game does not need to sell a blockbuster number of units to be merited a financial success. Many independents don't spend resources trying to develop features that will cater to the mass market. Often, they spend their resources on what they feel is important for their game, and let their target audience be gamers with the same tastes.

Alternative Funding

  • Once you significantly reduce the amount of resources you need to develop a game, the number of opportunities to find different methods of funding rise exponentially.

Get Personal

  • If you are developing something that strikes a chord with gamers, the community surrounding your game can be a very powerful and free (or cheap) resource. Put your fans in charge of marketing your game, use them as moderators, allow them to create content.
