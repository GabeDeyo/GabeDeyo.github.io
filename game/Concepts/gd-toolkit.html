Adaptive Soundtracks

  • Dynamic Soundtracks can not only add character and texture to a game, but can also lend positive or negative feedback to the player.

  • About the bear minimum soundtracks nowadays will at least give you a simple but dynamic soundtrack. For example, the tone changes when in combat and back to calm when the last enemy dies. More nuanced games will add a little more, such as RDR's interweaving tracks between country transitions.

  • Analyze Nintendos changing soundtracks between different locations. Each track reflects the players current area. The speed and layers increase during specific events. Ex.. when a train is speeding up/slowing down the track speed follows. This can be seen in almost all of their work.

  • Be on the lookout for transitional events that would be suited for a dynamic track. The soundtrack should not hinder the gameplay in any way. However it should make your game feel better layered and cinematic. Should add character to the play-style of your game. Mario = Playful, Zelda = Adventureous

  • Adaptive soundtracks should not only add this secondary texture to the game, but can help out in many cases. Ex.. when a successful solution is found in Portal 2, a layer is added onto the track to signify to the player they are heading in the right direction.

Sequence Breaking - Analyzing Toki Tori 2

  • Sequence Breaking can be utilized to help a world feel more organic and available.

  • Metroidvania games generally feature a large interconnected world map the player can explore, though access to parts of the world is often limited by doors or other portals that can only be opened after the player has acquired special items, tools, weapons or abilities within the game.

  • The design of these Metroidvania games is usually hindered however because the goal is to make the player feel like they are on a completely open path of their choosing , when really they are on a linear path dictated by the items and power ups in their arsenal.

  • TT2 has an interesting design because they have a whole system based strictly on two core gamplay moves. There are other things the player can do that don't directly effect gameplay such as veiwing a map or taking a picture. Any puzzle in the game can be solved with just a combination and knowledge of these two core moves.

  • The tutorial also benefits from these simple moves because it gives the player the chance to learn and digest these moves before being able to move on. The interesting mechanic that this may propose is that there may be several paths through tutorial or puzzle sections that the player wouldn't know how to even solve yet, but going back would easily notice. An experienced player could potentially bypass the tutorial all-together due to an advanced solution that a novice would miss.

  • This technique gives trust to your players without handholding and big compasses. It really utilizes the intellegence of the player in a satisfying way.

  • *Be Careful - Not every Metroidvania should use this technique. If the system is not carefully designed, the player could miss information, get stuck, or get frustrated.

Theme & Mechanics (FarCry 2/4)

  • By defining the mechanics of a game, thematics can follow along and define the tone of that game as well.

  • When analyzing two games from the same series, Far Cry 2 and 4, we can't help but notice that these feel like two completely different games. One of the core differences between these games is their mechanics. In 2, weapons and cars break down easily leaving the player vulnerable and weak in frequent intervals. In 4, many weaknesses are taken away and the player is left to roam the lands as a powerful player.

  • One core system that changes how players approach the game is how saving works. In 2, there are rarely safe places to save and are sparce enough that the player needs to play conservativley and with a focus on survival. If you're not careful, you can lose tons of time if you die between a save point in FarCry 2.
    Compared to FarCry 4, there is a much more lenient saving system where there is almost no punishmet for death at all.

  • The guns in each game are approached quite differently as well. The player is given a massive variety of guns in FarCry 4 and given silenced weapons from pretty much the start. Weapons in FarCry 2 have the ability to degrade and get worse over time. They can also jam at the worst times and there aren't a ton of options here. Not to mention the only silenced weapon in the game is a dart gun which is the most degradable weapons in the game and only holds a handful of darts.

Invisible Tutorial

  • Big Idea
    Through clever level design and building complexity, a player can be intruduced cleverly to any sort of mechanic in a game without losing the immersion.

  • Through out history, games have been shoving tutorial screen in front of it's players faces without remorse. A game like Dead Space more or less ruins it's own immersion when it tells the players how to fight the enemies 3 times, then simply displays the same rule in a pop-up! We analyze Half-Life 2 to see where genious tutorials are applied.

  • First thing to note is that there are pretty much no pop-ups and no real instructions on how to play the game.
    When first engaging one mechanic, the game allows you to visualize or experience it safely, blocks your progress with a situation where you need to use it, and then gives you the opportunity to learn and use it safely. In this order.
    Then we see the same concept being used when introducing enemies or obstacles. It is seperated from the player so they are not in immediate danger, shows the enemy/obstacle mechanic in action, and then lets the player confront it in a simple confrontation.

  • One important aspect of these tutorials are that they are all continuous and engaging parts of the story. They never take the player out of the immersion and are presented in clean encapsulated sections.

  • It is also important to note that many of these mechanics are also presented to the player through level design and layers. When first getting introduced to a puzzle mechanic, there are always hints in the game world to help guide the player towards the solution. Then after getting familiar with that core principle, the game starts adding layers of complexity onto that puzzle, although, one layer by one and never overloading them with mechanics.

Game Feel & Juice

  • Game feel is a largely abstract and invisible art critical to making great platform/action games.

  • Unfortunatly this feeling is hard to pin-point and usually depends on each specific game. It should help a player feel like they are truly interacting with and touching the game world.

  • Some pretty good examples of this in-game technique can be seen through screen shake, precice time manipulation, or almost anything to give the player feedback that their character is interacting with the world or enemies. Sound effects can play a big part in game feel as well (Adaptive Sounds)

  • Sound effects can play a big part in game feel as well ( Adaptive Sounds). Simple examples of this could be tones rising with each subsequent collection of points, or simply realistic gun shot sounds rather than bland shots.

  • The camera can play a huge role in game feel too. It should have an intelligence about it and move around the world in a unique manner.

  • Ultimately, game, feel is all about doubling down on the core of your gameplay and perfecting those mechanics. It takes a lot of polish and tinkering to make it work but the end result is much better.

4 Step Level Design - Analyzing Super Mario 3D World

  • This section looks at a Japanese poem structure technique that allows players to be introduced to a mechanic, develop it, twist it, and embed that mechanic in the players arsenal.

  • Introduction - In the first stage, safely introduce the mechanic to the player where they would not be punished for failure.

  • Develop - In the second stage, use the mechanic again in a harder system where they may have to get better until they can pass.

  • Twist - In the third stage, turn the concept on its head to help solidify the players mastery or let them think about the concept more deeply.

  • Conclusion - In the final stage, give the player a chance to really show off their newly honed skills with a flagpole type finish. Could be a hard way to finish the level, or use those skills in a boss fight.

  • This technique is helpful in the long run because the game can re-introduce concepts introduced earlier and be confident the player will remember and utilize the same skills as they used previously.

  • Be sure to let a mechanic mature so the player has adequate time to learn and perfect it. Also don't throw too many mechanics at a player at once, unless they have learned the previous mechanics and your just twisting the current mechanic.

Redesigning Death

  • Death can be used as an intricate and unique component in a game that can shape a game and its core meaning.

  • For many years, death has been executed in relatively the same way. Player dies. Black screen. Rewind time. Try again.
    Mostly, the only thing thats changed is games are even more lenient.

  • There are some solutions however. Many rogue like games will completely change when the player dies. Spelunky and others change their dungeons before setting you out again or have checkpoints you can access with a risk of missing crucial early items. Rogue Legacy even changes the characters lineage to help get a different playstyle.

  • Some even more in-depth death mechanics we can see today is Shadow of Mordor. The Nemesis System changes the core enemy groups on a players death. In this case, the player may get killed, but the enemy that kills the player really changes the enemy group dynamic.

  • When killing a player, don't take everything away from them but give them the opportunity to risk winning their loot back. Some games even make you face previous versions of yourself in order to take regain your pride.

Getting Lost - Analysis of Axiom Verge

  • There is a hidden linear progression in many Metroidvanias and other genres that can steer a player in the correct direction without halting their curiosity and exploration.

  • The Metroidvania formula is a strong design concept that has ways of going right and wrong. Axiom Verge is one such game that brings back the nostolgic roots of great Metroidvania games.

  • One great rule that AV abides by is that it never tells you where to go, and this rule can be applied to many other genres as well. While AV gives the player the illusion of complete freedom, it does use some interesting tricks to help the player stay on the recommended path.

  • We can see this when a player comes to an important point that is visually sealed in their brain, however they have no way at that point of passing. When a user gets the tool needed to get through, that visual memory will easily come back to the player in a AHAA! moment. Gives them something to be excited about coming back to.

  • Another trick AV uses is to give a player more than one option in progressing or doubling down on that mechanic to help show them the possibilities. Giving a user multiple options to the same result multiplies the chance that they will understand and successfully use the mechanic.

  • In order to reduce unecessary backtracking when in a critical puzzle solving moment or important mechanic tutorial, use temporary barriers to allow the player to focus on the key concept in that location. This helps users solidify their understanding of a mechanic.

  • Don't completely rule out backtracking however. Allow the player to explore and even discover crazy new things with their awesome new powers. Powerful and experienced players should be able to breeze through old parts of the game with new things to discover and some incentive to do so. Backtracking should be just as fun as going through the first time.

Telling Stories with Systems

  • Big Idea

  • Many Story Telling games are hindered by their limitations to freely allow the user to fully explore an open narrative. They use tricks to bring the player back on the core store line, or play out a scene with just different settings or characters.

  • However, many other games, not even in a story telling genre reveal systems that tell better and more personal stories that a fixed story line could. When systems can interact with each other in unique ways, it gives the player a chance to see events that probably other players wouldn't.

Dynamic Difficulty

  • Big Idea
    By secretly implementing a dynamic difficulty system, a designer can inflict the sweet spot of flow in their players and make the experience feel perfectly suited to each players skill level.

  • After analyzing Capcoms Resident Evil 4, lets look at some mechanics that actually change when the player is not doing too well.
      • Enemies don't rush as quick.
      • More loot found in crates and more frequent.
      • Enemies are easier to take down.
      • Some enemies won't even show up.
    There are others, but each independently changed based on player skill.

  • Easily the biggest advantages to a dynamic difficulty system is setting the player into flow. In this state, the player experiences the game with enough difficulty where it's not too boring and easy, and not too frustrating and hard.
    When other games force the player to select a difficulty at the beginning of the game or change it manually, the player may not experience the game in the intended way.

  • So while the dynamic difficulty can allow the player to progress while being properly challenged, it's still not the best advantage of the system. The game NEVER tells you that there is even a mechanic in place for that use. Many games do however and some even mock or cheat a player through when they don't play well. Resident Evil 4 never gives the sense that a player is doing bad and never cheats them out of that rewarding progress.

Puzzle Solving vs Problem Solving

  • There is a big difference in games between FINDING and INVENTING a solution to a problem.

  • Typical puzzle games offer one solution where more clever games, such as InfiniFactory offer essentially unlimited solutions.

  • One genious InfiniFactory imparts is that it compares the effectivness of your solution with other successful players. Being that there are many solutions, it is satisfying to see your solution at the top of the leaderboards.

  • Another great option with this design is the potential for re-playability. It is fairly pointless to re-play the solutions to games with very limited puzzle solving options. With unlimited options, the game can always offer the chance to come back and work on a better solution.

  • These problem ideas can actually simple to find in real world problems. Look at interesting issues that people face, and find a way to implement into a game like system. At this point, the game could even be offered as a educational experience or simulation type game.

Scourge of Scale

  • Big Idea
    Bigger isn't always better. Many important design features are lost when a game grows beyond the scope of it's core ideas.

  • When analyzing the Batman Arkham games, there is an arguable winner. Arkham Asylum is almost always the popular choice among critics even though it's size and scale pale in comparison to its predecessors. The map is smaller, there are less characters, less mechanics, and really just smaller in general. So why do people prefer this game over the bigger, bulkier others?

  • The first thing to notice is that the mechanics and combat don't actually benefit from being set in a larger game space. In fact, they seem to work much better in the purpose built levels of Arkham than the open world of the others.

  • When considering a sandbox, it should make sense to only put sandbox elements in that world, otherwise those elements can get lost in the vastness. Then it will feel like the game only truly takes place in these specific sections and lifeless between.

  • One of the biggest victims of a large game world is story. Narrative can get completely lost when the world offers too many distractions. In these games, the narrative is too often interupted by side-quests and small things to do.
    Arkham Asylum was great because the story was able to be paced evenly and there was still great gameplay to follow it up even if there was less features. A key principle in game design is quality over quantity. Work out extremely well polished mechanics rather than a plethora of okay mechanics.

Is The Swindle the Perfect Heist Game?

  • Big Idea

  • What's to stop gamers, during a heist gone very bad, to simply take the fall and restarting to the previous checkpoint. There is no punishment or incentive for not taking the easy way out. Lets analyze The Swindle to see how they created the perfect heist game.

  • The Swindle creates amazing tension within the player by setting the stakes very high. A slip up in this game could cost you in the end, and yet the game pushes and lures the player toward risky situations in order to get that sweet sweet loot.

  • The Swindle only provides a limited amount of mechanics which you can use to progress through the games heists. Getting loot allows you to upgrade those mechanics, unlock more heists, and gain access to the last heist, "The Swindle". But it's perfection comes from two design features.

  • 1. There is only 100 days (heists) that you get to complete "The Swindle". If you don't make it, thats game over.
    2. Also the character recieves certain bonuses based on how their performance for each heist went.

Point & Click Puzzle Design

  • Big Idea

  • Rule 1. Provide clear goals. Players should always know what their long and short term goals are so they don't feel lost.

  • Rule 2. Sign posting. Posting clear sign posting is a great way to subtly guiding the player. The technique doesn't have to be include literal signs, but clever clues to the goal. Carefully design the signs so they don't feel to easy or hard.

  • Rule 3. Feedback. A puzzle should help solve itself. Nudging a player towards the goal can be less infiriating.

Lessons from a Real Time Turnbase

  • Embracing strong features, whether designed or not, can result it unique and amibitious cross-over genres.

  • Lesson 1. Even the most diseparate genres of games can come together.
    We can see this done well in Nova 111 where real-time actions and turn-based actions can be used together in a strategic manner. Many environmental object react to the players actions in real time and behave like they would normally, where enemies can only act in turn based style which is completely based on user interaction.
    A strategic way to use this mechanic is by dropping a stalagtite from the cieling, and luring an enemy into it's path by progressing the turn quickly. This can also make gameplay tricky for the player when they are put in a tight position and need to act quickly.
    Its a great push and pull between real-time actions and turn based strategy.

  • Lesson 2.Be serendipitous towards interesting developmnets of game development.
    Several game changing features were discovered through errors or glitches in the development cycle. If the feature makes sense or can be built upon, embrace it.

  • Lesson 3.If some feature is at the core of your game, it needs to shine through in every aspect. Feature it, build on it, twist it, and perfect it.

Mechanics of Movement

  • The way a user controls a character and the way a character moves/behaves should help define a character and connect a user with its player.

  • Movement should not be a declining automated process where the user doesn't even need to think about it. It should be a thoughtfull and skillful mechanic which rewards the player for correctly managing it's system.

  • A player should feel as though they are behaving like the character, not just controlling the character. Character response should be fluid and instantaneous. There shouldn't be any disconnect between how the user interacts with a character, and how the character behaves.

How "Her Story" Works

  • There is a artful design aspect where players are carefully guided through a complicated narrative and making it feel like a completely open story.

  • Her Story is presented as a non-linear narrative story that makes the player feel like they are unravelling the story piece by piece. However, behind the scenes, this game uses clever techniques to keep the player on a linear track.

  • The designer is always aware however, of the location in the narrative story where the player is. And is always prepared to halt the player from skipping the entire story when necessary.
    In some locations, the player is actually rewarded for being clever, and can skip small portions where less clever players would be progressing slowly. An important part of this game is not solving the end story, but actually understanding the story fully.

How Jonathan Blow Designs Puzzles

  • For Jonathan Blow, a puzzle is never a puzzle. It's a way for a player and designer to communicate understanding between the two.

  • For his first game, Braid, Jon started with a Mechanic. In this case it was the ability to rewind time for a nearly unlimited amount of time.
    Then he add a Rule which slightly changed the main mechanic of the game. The first rule we encounter is that some select object won't be effected by the rewind effect. This leads to some interesting and clever puzzles.
    Nearly every level after this first rule's introduction, is some variation of manipulating that main mechanic.

  • In his second game, The Witness, he explores the same development psyche. Blow establishes a main mechanic and builds upon that with rules through exploration and testing.

  • One of the most brilliant features of his design is that the rules fully utilize the full potential of their uniqness. If there is a strange consequence of the rule, there is surely a puzzle based on this consequence. He also cleverly guides you to try out solutions again with misdirection, only to show you that it is incorrect and you should try something else.

  • He gives the players a way to fully explore and understand the core system of a rule or mechanic before letting you continue. Then implements those base concepts in later levels twisted by those levels new rule.

Spelunky Puzzles

  • Spelunky proves that random and authored content doesn't have to lead to soulless levels. And learning underlying mechanics can make random levels interesting to play.

  • Spelunky's designer has found the perfect balance between Random and Authored content. To understand how these levels are created, we're going to walk through the process from head to toe to get an idea.

  • The design of a leve starts out with 16 rooms in a 4x4 grid. It randomly picks a room in the top row to be the entrance. From the entrance, a path is selected to the left, right, or bottom of that room and repeated until we get to the lowest level. If the path tries to go down when on the bottom row, an exit is created.
    It's important to note that this dedicated path will always allow a player to move through it without needing any other tools. Anything room not on the path is not garunteed to be a viable path.

  • After the level path is created, each room is given a random template. Some of these templates are fully crafted by the designer and usually signify a common room in the levels, where other rooms are fully crafted using random placment of blocks.
    Once rooms are finished, a dice is rolled to place enemies, items, and gems around the rooms. This isn't completely random because we want some things to spawn more than other at specific locations and differently later on.

  • With fixed and random content, any levels design will have enough variation and difference between the last, that the player can't get accustomed to it's tricks. The designer also gives players a risk vs reward system that allows players to risk their safety to get a usually helpful reward. But it's also a guessing game to find out whether or not that reward will be worth any of the risk.
