Adaptive Soundtracks

  • Dynamic Soundtracks can not only add character and texture to a game, but can also lend positive or negative feedback to the player.

  • About the bear minimum soundtracks nowadays will at least give you a simple but dynamic soundtrack. For example, the tone changes when in combat and back to calm when the last enemy dies. More nuanced games will add a little more, such as RDR's interweaving tracks between country transitions.

  • Analyze Nintendos changing soundtracks between different locations. Each track reflects the players current area. The speed and layers increase during specific events. Ex.. when a train is speeding up/slowing down the track speed follows. This can be seen in almost all of their work.

  • Be on the lookout for transitional events that would be suited for a dynamic track. The soundtrack should not hinder the gameplay in any way. However it should make your game feel better layered and cinematic. Should add character to the play-style of your game. Mario = Playful, Zelda = Adventureous

  • Adaptive soundtracks should not only add this secondary texture to the game, but can help out in many cases. Ex.. when a successful solution is found in Portal 2, a layer is added onto the track to signify to the player they are heading in the right direction.

Sequence Breaking - Analyzing Toki Tori 2

  • Sequence Breaking can be utilized to help a world feel more organic and available.

  • Metroidvania games generally feature a large interconnected world map the player can explore, though access to parts of the world is often limited by doors or other portals that can only be opened after the player has acquired special items, tools, weapons or abilities within the game.

  • The design of these Metroidvania games is usually hindered however because the goal is to make the player feel like they are on a completely open path of their choosing , when really they are on a linear path dictated by the items and power ups in their arsenal.

  • TT2 has an interesting design because they have a whole system based strictly on two core gamplay moves. There are other things the player can do that don't directly effect gameplay such as veiwing a map or taking a picture. Any puzzle in the game can be solved with just a combination and knowledge of these two core moves.

  • The tutorial also benefits from these simple moves because it gives the player the chance to learn and digest these moves before being able to move on. The interesting mechanic that this may propose is that there may be several paths through tutorial or puzzle sections that the player wouldn't know how to even solve yet, but going back would easily notice. An experienced player could potentially bypass the tutorial all-together due to an advanced solution that a novice would miss.

  • This technique gives trust to your players without handholding and big compasses. It really utilizes the intellegence of the player in a satisfying way.

  • *Be Careful - Not every Metroidvania should use this technique. If the system is not carefully designed, the player could miss information, get stuck, or get frustrated.

Game Feel & Juice

  • Game feel is a largely abstract and invisible art critical to making great platform/action games.

  • Unfortunatly this feeling is hard to pin-point and usually depends on each specific game. It should help a player feel like they are truly interacting with and touching the game world.

  • Some pretty good examples of this in-game technique can be seen through screen shake, precice time manipulation, or almost anything to give the player feedback that their character is interacting with the world or enemies. Sound effects can play a big part in game feel as well (Adaptive Sounds)

  • Sound effects can play a big part in game feel as well (Adaptive Sounds). Simple examples of this could be tones rising with each subsequent collection of points, or simply realistic gun shot sounds rather than bland shots.

  • The camera can play a huge role in game feel too. It should have an intelligence about it and move around the world in a unique manner.

  • Ultimately, game, feel is all about doubling down on the core of your gameplay and perfecting those mechanics. It takes a lot of polish and tinkering to make it work but the end result is much better.

4 Step Level Design - Analyzing Super Mario 3D World

  • This section looks at a Japanese poem structure technique that allows players to be introduced to a mechanic, develop it, twist it, and embed that mechanic in the players arsenal.

  • Introduction - In the first stage, safely introduce the mechanic to the player where they would not be punished for failure.

  • Develop - In the second stage, use the mechanic again in a harder system where they may have to get better until they can pass.

  • Twist - In the third stage, turn the concept on its head to help solidify the players mastery or let them think about the concept more deeply.

  • Conclusion - In the final stage, give the player a chance to really show off their newly honed skills with a flagpole type finish. Could be a hard way to finish the level, or use those skills in a boss fight.

  • This technique is helpful in the long run because the game can re-introduce concepts introduced earlier and be confident the player will remember and utilize the same skills as they used previously.

  • Be sure to let a mechanic mature so the player has adequate time to learn and perfect it. Also don't throw too many mechanics at a player at once, unless they have learned the previous mechanics and your just twisting the current mechanic.

Redesigning Death

  • Death can be used as an intricate and unique component in a game that can shape a game and its core meaning.

  • For many years, death has been executed in relatively the same way. Player dies. Black screen. Rewind time. Try again.
    Mostly, the only thing thats changed is games are even more lenient.

  • There are some solutions however. Many rogue like games will completely change when the player dies. Spelunky and others change their dungeons before setting you out again or have checkpoints you can access with a risk of missing crucial early items. Rogue Legacy even changes the characters lineage to help get a different playstyle.

  • Some even more in-depth death mechanics we can see today is Shadow of Mordor. The Nemesis System changes the core enemy groups on a players death. In this case, the player may get killed, but the enemy that kills the player really changes the enemy group dynamic.

  • When killing a player, don't take everything away from them but give them the opportunity to risk winning their loot back. Some games even make you face previous versions of yourself in order to take regain your pride.

Getting Lost - Analysis of Axiom Verge

  • There is a hidden linear progression in many Metroidvanias and other genres that can steer a player in the correct direction without halting their curiosity and exploration.

  • The Metroidvania formula is a strong design concept that has ways of going right and wrong. Axiom Verge is one such game that brings back the nostolgic roots of great Metroidvania games.

  • One great rule that AV abides by is that it never tells you where to go, and this rule can be applied to many other genres as well. While AV gives the player the illusion of complete freedom, it does use some interesting tricks to help the player stay on the recommended path.

  • We can see this when a player comes to an important point that is visually sealed in their brain, however they have no way at that point of passing. When a user gets the tool needed to get through, that visual memory will easily come back to the player in a AHAA! moment. Gives them something to be excited about coming back to.

  • Another trick AV uses is to give a player more than one option in progressing or doubling down on that mechanic to help show them the possibilities. Giving a user multiple options to the same result multiplies the chance that they will understand and successfully use the mechanic.

  • In order to reduce unecessary backtracking when in a critical puzzle solving moment or important mechanic tutorial, use temporary barriers to allow the player to focus on the key concept in that location. This helps users solidify their understanding of a mechanic.

  • Don't completely rule out backtracking however. Allow the player to explore and even discover crazy new things with their awesome new powers. Powerful and experienced players should be able to breeze through old parts of the game with new things to discover and some incentive to do so. Backtracking should be just as fun as going through the first time.

Telling Stories with Systems

  • Big Idea

  • Many Story Telling games are hindered by their limitations to freely allow the user to fully explore an open narrative. They use tricks to bring the player back on the core store line, or play out a scene with just different settings or characters.

  • However, many other games, not even in a story telling genre reveal systems that tell better and more personal stories that a fixed story line could. When systems can interact with each other in unique ways, it gives the player a chance to see events that probably other players wouldn't.
