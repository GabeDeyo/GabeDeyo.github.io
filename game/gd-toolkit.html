Adaptive Soundtracks

  • Dynamic Soundtracks can not only add character and texture to a game, but can also lend positive or negative feedback to the player.

  • About the bear minimum soundtracks nowadays will at least give you a simple but dynamic soundtrack. For example, the tone changes when in combat and back to calm when the last enemy dies. More nuanced games will add a little more, such as RDR's interweaving tracks between country transitions.

  • Analyze Nintendos changing soundtracks between different locations. Each track reflects the players current area. The speed and layers increase during specific events. Ex.. when a train is speeding up/slowing down the track speed follows. This can be seen in almost all of their work.

  • Be on the lookout for transitional events that would be suited for a dynamic track. The soundtrack should not hinder the gameplay in any way. However it should make your game feel better layered and cinematic. Should add character to the play-style of your game. Mario = Playful, Zelda = Adventureous

  • Adaptive soundtracks should not only add this secondary texture to the game, but can help out in many cases. Ex.. when a successful solution is found in Portal 2, a layer is added onto the track to signify to the player they are heading in the right direction.

Sequence Breaking - Analyzing Toki Tori 2

  • Sequence Breaking can be utilized to help a world feel more organic and available.

  • Metroidvania games generally feature a large interconnected world map the player can explore, though access to parts of the world is often limited by doors or other portals that can only be opened after the player has acquired special items, tools, weapons or abilities within the game.

  • The design of these Metroidvania games is usually hindered however because the goal is to make the player feel like they are on a completely open path of their choosing , when really they are on a linear path dictated by the items and power ups in their arsenal.

  • TT2 has an interesting design because they have a whole system based strictly on two core gamplay moves. There are other things the player can do that don't directly effect gameplay such as veiwing a map or taking a picture. Any puzzle in the game can be solved with just a combination and knowledge of these two core moves.

  • The tutorial also benefits from these simple moves because it gives the player the chance to learn and digest these moves before being able to move on. The interesting mechanic that this may propose is that there may be several paths through tutorial or puzzle sections that the player wouldn't know how to even solve yet, but going back would easily notice. An experienced player could potentially bypass the tutorial all-together due to an advanced solution that a novice would miss.

  • This technique gives trust to your players without handholding and big compasses. It really utilizes the intellegence of the player in a satisfying way.

  • *Be Careful - Not every Metroidvania should use this technique. If the system is not carefully designed, the player could miss information, get stuck, or get frustrated.
